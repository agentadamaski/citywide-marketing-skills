## 1. Overview — Who We Are Targeting

Zoopro's current target investor sits in one of two primary profiles. Both are warm — they know the BTL model is under pressure, but they haven't yet found or fully understood the supported housing alternative.

**Primary investment range: £100,000 – £500,000**

This is capital that is:
- Already working (existing portfolio or business profits)
- Looking for a better home — not sitting idle
- Sophisticated enough to evaluate structure, not just headline yield
- Not speculative — these investors want security, not a gamble

---

## 2. The Two Primary Profiles

### Profile A — The Professional Property Investor

**Who they are:**
An established property investor with an existing portfolio — typically 3–15 properties. Has been investing for 5+ years. Understands yield, void risk, and compliance from lived experience. May have a letting agent managing their portfolio, or may self-manage.

**Financial profile:**
- Net investable capital: £100k–£500k available for deployment
- Existing portfolio: likely mix of residential BTL, some HMO
- Income: property income + often a separate business or employment
- Business revenue: typically £100k+ per annum

**Mindset:**
- Experienced — they've seen market cycles
- Sceptical of hype — they've been burned by inflated yield claims before
- Systems-oriented — they want a model that works without constant attention
- Time-poor — managing a portfolio alongside other commitments
- Long-term thinker — not looking for a quick flip

**What's happening in their world right now:**
- The Renters Reform Act and its compliance burden is increasing cost and complexity
- Void rates are higher than they were 3–5 years ago
- Section 21 removal has shifted the risk balance toward tenants
- Mortgage rate increases have compressed net yields on leveraged BTL
- They're increasingly asking: "Is this worth the hassle?"

**What they're looking for:**
- A model that delivers comparable or better yields with less active management
- Structural security — lease-backed, not tenancy-based
- A trusted partner who understands the space — not a platform flogging deals
- Clear numbers they can model — not vague promises
- A simple next step that doesn't feel like a high-pressure sales funnel

**What they are not looking for:**
- Another buy-to-let deal
- A complicated scheme they need a solicitor to explain
- Someone who doesn't understand their existing portfolio context
- Marketing language that talks down to them

**Where they spend time:**
- LinkedIn (professional network, industry content)
- Property investor forums and communities
- WhatsApp groups (informal peer networks)
- Podcast listeners — property, business, investing
- Letting agent relationships (trust-based, long-standing)

**How they make decisions:**
- Due diligence first — they research before they engage
- Peer validation matters — word of mouth and referral carries significant weight
- They want to speak to someone before committing — not click a button
- Timeline: 4–12 weeks from first touch to serious conversation
- Touchpoints required: 10–25 before a meaningful conversation

---

### Profile B — The BTL Landlord Diversifying

**Who they are:**
A landlord with an existing residential BTL portfolio — typically 2–8 properties. May not consider themselves a "professional investor" but has accumulated assets over time. Likely manages their own properties or uses a local agent. Feeling the regulatory squeeze more acutely than Profile A.

**Financial profile:**
- Net investable capital: £500k–£150k (often equity released or savings)
- Existing portfolio: primarily residential AST
- Income: mix of rental income and employment or business
- Less financially sophisticated than Profile A but highly motivated by security

**Mindset:**
- Frustrated — regulation has made their existing model harder and more stressful
- Cautious — they've worked hard for what they have and don't want to lose it
- Open to change — but needs it explained clearly and simply
- Values relationships over platforms — they want to deal with a person
- May have heard of supported housing but has misconceptions about risk

**What's happening in their world right now:**
- Renters Reform Act has made Section 21 eviction much harder
- Awaab's Law and EPC requirements increasing compliance burden
- Some are considering selling — but don't want to exit the asset class entirely
- Looking for a way to keep the assets but remove the landlord headaches
- May be speaking to their letting agent about options

**What they're looking for:**
- A way to protect their asset while removing tenant management
- Something that makes financial sense without requiring complex understanding
- Someone patient enough to explain the model clearly
- Reassurance that this is legitimate and compliant
- A clear, low-pressure next step

**What they are not looking for:**
- Jargon or complexity
- High-pressure sales
- Anything that feels risky or unregulated
- To be talked at rather than talked with

**Where they spend time:**
- Local letting agent relationships — this is the primary referral route for this profile
- WhatsApp (peer groups, agent communications)
- Facebook property groups (less sophisticated digital behaviour)
- Word of mouth from other landlords

**How they make decisions:**
- Trust-based — they need to know and like you before proceeding
- Heavily influenced by who referred them
- Slower decision cycle: 4–16 weeks
- Touchpoints required: 15–30 before serious conversation
- Often needs a face-to-face or phone call to feel confident

## 3. Shared Audience Intelligence

Despite their differences, both profiles share these characteristics:

**Demographics:**
- Age range: typically 35–60
- UK-based, predominantly England
- Mix of ethnicities — BME community well-represented in Zoopro's network
- Likely homeowners, often family-oriented
- Business owners (take-aways, restaurants, convivence stores, SME's)
- Values financial independence and legacy wealth

**Emotional drivers:**
- Security over speculation — they want stable, predictable income
- Control — they want to understand what they're investing in
- Legacy — building something that lasts, not chasing short-term gains
- Freedom — removing the active management burden from their lives
- Ethics — increasingly concerned about where their capital goes

**Shared fears:**
- Being misled by yield claims that don't stack up
- Investing in something they don't fully understand
- Losing control of their asset
- Regulatory risk — "what if the rules change again?"
- Working with someone who disappears after the deal is done

**Shared values:**
- Transparency — show me the numbers, all of them
- Competence — you clearly know what you're doing
- Integrity — you'll tell me when something isn't right for me
- Relationship — this is a long-term partnership, not a transaction

## 4. Awareness Level — Warm but Uninformed

**Current state:**
Both profiles are warm. They know the BTL model is under pressure. They are open to alternatives. But they have not been properly introduced to the supported housing lease model.

**Common misconceptions to address:**
- "Supported housing means difficult tenants and more risk" — wrong. Risk depends on lease structure and provider quality, not tenant type.
- "It's not regulated properly" — wrong. The strongest models are compliance-led, commercially leased, and provider-backed.
- "It's only for specialist investors" — wrong. The model is designed for capital partners, not property managers.
- "The yields are too good to be true" — understandable. This must be addressed with specific numbers and transparent deal structures, not dismissed.

**The journey we need to take them on:**
1. Acknowledge the reality — BTL is harder than it was
2. Introduce the reframe — there's a different model that separates the asset from the management
3. Explain the mechanism — commercial lease, vetted provider, structured income
4. Provide proof — specific yields, lease terms, provider vetting process
5. Remove risk from the next step — join the waiting list, no commitment

## 5. Top Objections — With Response Angles

### "Is the yield really 8–12%? That sounds too high."

**What's behind it:** Previous experience of inflated claims in property investment marketing.

**Response angle:**
Never dismiss the scepticism — validate it. Then provide specificity.
- "That's a fair question — and you're right to push on it."
- "8–12% is the range across current supported housing deals. It depends on property type, location, lease length, and provider. We'll show you the deal structure, not just the headline number."
- "The yield is higher than standard BTL because the risk profile is different — you're not managing tenants, you're holding a commercial lease. The premium reflects the model, not inflated marketing."

**Never say:** "Yes, guaranteed." **Always say:** "Typically 8–12%, subject to deal structure and due diligence."

---

### "How secure is the lease? What happens if the provider can't pay?"

**What's behind it:** Legitimate concern about counter-party risk — especially for Profile B who values security above all.

**Response angle:**
- This is the right question and shows a sophisticated investor.
- "The lease is a commercial contract — it has the same legal standing as a commercial property lease. Provider vetting is central to what Zoopro does."
- "We don't just match any investor with any provider. We assess provider financial stability, track record, regulatory compliance, and lease history."
- "If a provider defaults, the asset remains yours. You're the freeholder. The provider doesn't own the property — they lease it."

**For Shariah-sensitive investors:** The commercial lease structure is not interest-based. The income is rental income from a legitimate commercial lease. Available to discuss in detail.

---

### "How does this work with the new regulation — Renters Reform Act, etc.?"

**What's behind it:** Regulatory fatigue. They've been affected before and don't want to be caught out again.

**Response angle:**
- "This is exactly why the supported housing lease model is gaining traction."
- "Commercial leases — which is what underpins Zoopro deals — sit outside residential tenancy regulation. The Renters Reform Act applies to ASTs. A commercial lease is a different legal instrument entirely."
- "This doesn't mean zero regulation — there are standards for supported housing providers, which is why provider vetting matters. But you're not subject to Section 21 abolition, EPC pressure on residential lettings, or Renters Reform compliance in the same way."

---

### "What's my exit strategy? Can I sell the property?"

**What's behind it:** Asset control anxiety — especially for Profile A who has built a portfolio and wants to maintain flexibility.

**Response angle:**
- "You own the freehold. You can sell at any point — the lease either transfers with the sale or you negotiate an exit with the provider."
- "A property with a long-term commercial lease to a regulated provider is often more attractive to buyers than a standard BTL — institutional buyers and other investors value the income security."
- "This isn't a lock-in. It's a structured income model with an underlying asset you control."

## 7. Qualifying Questions

Use these when speaking to or messaging a potential investor to determine profile and readiness:

**For Profile A:**
- "How many properties are you currently managing, and how are you finding the compliance side of things?"
- "Are you looking to grow your portfolio, or protect and simplify what you already have?"
- "Have you looked at any alternatives to standard AST tenancies for your existing properties?"

**For Profile B:**
- "How long have you been a landlord, and how is it feeling at the moment with all the regulatory changes?"
- "Are you thinking about your portfolio long-term, or are you starting to consider your options?"
- "Has your letting agent mentioned anything about alternative models?"

**Both profiles:**
- "What's your typical investment range for a new opportunity?"
- "How important is hands-off management to you versus maximising yield?"
- "Is Shariah compliance a consideration for you?"

## 8. Messaging Calibration by Profile

### Profile A — Peer-Level Messaging
Tone: Investor-to-investor. Respect their experience. Don't explain things they already know.
- Lead with the model, not the education
- Use specific numbers early — they'll disengage if you're vague
- Acknowledge the BTL pressure without being preachy about it
- Position Zoopro as a platform built for operators, not beginners

### Profile B — Patient, Trust-Building Messaging
Tone: Warm, clear, unhurried. Don't overwhelm. Build confidence gradually.
- Start with the problem they recognise (landlord stress, compliance burden)
- Introduce the reframe gently — "what if you kept the asset but removed the management?"
- Use analogies and simple language before introducing structure details
- Make the next step feel safe and low-commitment
- Let the relationship build before asking for anything
